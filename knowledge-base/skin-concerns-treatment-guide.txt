Glow Beauty Co. — Skin Concerns Treatment Guide (Evidence-Based)

Purpose
Conventional, dermatologist-aligned guidance for common skin concerns. Recommend products from catalog that match these ingredient and routine principles.

Acne and Breakout Prevention
Types: blackheads, whiteheads, papules, pustules; prevention focuses on pore clarity and oil regulation.
First-line over-the-counter actives: salicylic acid (BHA) 0.5-2% for pore exfoliation; benzoyl peroxide 2.5-5% for antibacterial action (start lower); niacinamide for oil and redness; adapalene is prescription-strength retinoid — do not claim OTC replaces dermatologist care for moderate-severe acne.
Routine priority for acne prevention:
1. Gentle cleanser (morning and night)
2. BHA leave-on treatment or niacinamide serum (night, start 2-3x weekly)
3. Oil-free non-comedogenic moisturizer (yes, oily acne skin still needs moisturizer)
4. SPF 30+ every morning (prevents post-acne dark marks)
Avoid: picking, harsh scrubs on inflamed lesions, coconut oil on acne-prone face, stacking BHA + retinol + benzoyl peroxide on the same night when starting.
When to escalate: painful cysts, acne not improving after 8-12 weeks of consistent OTC routine, scarring, or emotional distress — see a dermatologist.

Post-Acne Dark Marks (PIH)
Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation fades faster with strict daily SPF, niacinamide, azelaic acid 10%, vitamin C, and optional alpha arbutin.
Avoid sun exposure on healing blemishes. Do not exfoliate aggressively over active inflammation.

Hyperpigmentation and Uneven Skin Tone
Evidence-supported actives: vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid 10-20% or stable derivatives), niacinamide, azelaic acid, alpha arbutin, tranexamic acid (serums), retinoids.
Critical: sunscreen every morning — treatment fails without UV protection.
Morning: antioxidant serum (vitamin C) → moisturizer → SPF.
Night: retinol or azelaic acid (alternate nights when beginning) → moisturizer.
Timeline: visible improvement often requires 8-12 weeks of consistent use.

Fine Lines and Wrinkles (Aging Prevention)
Gold-standard preventive ingredients: retinoids (retinol OTC, start low 0.2-0.3%), peptides, vitamin C, SPF.
Retinol protocol: 2 nights per week for 2 weeks, then every other night if tolerated; always use SPF by day.
Support with: hyaluronic acid for hydration, ceramides for barrier, eye cream with peptides or retinol if tolerated.
Avoid: tanning, smoking (educate generally), skipping sunscreen when using retinol.

Dehydration vs Dry Skin
Dehydration = lack of water in skin (can happen on oily skin). Dry skin = lack of oil/lipids.
Treatment for dehydration: hyaluronic acid on damp skin, glycerin, hydrating toners, then seal with moisturizer.
Treatment for dry skin: ceramides, squalane, richer creams, avoid stripping cleansers.
Both need: reduce hot water exposure, humidifier in dry climates optional.

Redness and Reactive Skin
Soothing ingredients: centella, allantoin, azelaic acid, niacinamide (lower % if reactive), colloidal oatmeal.
Mineral sunscreen often better tolerated than chemical filters for reactive skin.
Avoid: menthol, eucalyptus, high-strength exfoliants, essential oil-heavy formulas.
If redness is persistent with bumps resembling rosacea, recommend dermatologist evaluation.

Enlarged Pores (Appearance)
Pores cannot shrink permanently, but appearance improves with: salicylic acid, niacinamide, retinol, consistent SPF, and oil control without over-drying.
Clay masks once weekly may help oily T-zone.

Dullness and Uneven Texture
Causes: dead cell buildup, dehydration, sun damage.
Approach: gentle chemical exfoliation (AHA like lactic or glycolic 5-10% for dry/normal; BHA for oily), vitamin C morning, retinol night, daily SPF.
Do not combine strong AHA and retinol on the same night when first starting.

Body Acne (Back and Chest)
Use: salicylic acid body wash, benzoyl peroxide wash (short contact), loose breathable fabrics, shower after sweating.
Avoid: heavy occlusive body oils on breakout areas.

Pregnancy and Nursing (General Caution)
Avoid retinoids, high-strength salicylic acid peels, and hydroquinone unless directed by a physician. Prioritize SPF, gentle cleansers, azelaic acid and niacinamide are often considered lower-risk options — customer should confirm with their doctor.

Safety Disclaimer
This guide supports product selection and education only. It does not diagnose, treat, or prescribe. Severe, painful, or worsening conditions require a licensed dermatologist.
